]]> Tamar, his wife, scurried around taking note of what was needed but not actually doing much except occasionally tending to the few women in the group. This was a bit of a treat as far as she was concerned. She could move around quietly, ears fully alert and listen to the stories these newcomers brought with them. Like walking, talking newspapers, they filled her mind with new things to share with her friends.

Yes, she was a gossip but Bina liked her. She did not mean to be threatening, even so, to Bina she was. Over the years of living and working with them, Tamar had tried to probe into her past.  To a woman like Tamar, not being allowed into this part of Bina’s life was irritating, even painful; and Bina knew it. The three of them had become more like family than employers and employee so this barrier was respectfully accepted by Tamar. Most of the time the older woman did not even think of it but every now and then she would launch into a new assault, trying her best to get some answers. Bina parried deftly each time by firing some questions of her own then quickly changing the subject. When that did not work she would make an excuse to leave the room. Tamar retreated every time.

Bina breathed out, releasing a slight tension which had crept in as thoughts of the past had crept back into her mind.

]]> Leo was desperate. He drove on through the mist, twisting the map in his free hand. It’s all Tilly’s fault, he thought. “Let’s get married in Tuscany!” he mimicked and winced as he drove on. 

At least HIS idea had some sense. He had arranged for them to have a day to themselves before the wedding. A sort of role-play adventure, a game.  It seemed like a simple, good idea, but his soon to be bride, Tilly, had a bad feeling about it.

“You want us to meet by chance? WHERE!”

“In an Italian country inn.” He handed her the map.

She looked down at it and then up at him. “How did you find this place?”

“Friends of mine stumbled on it a few years ago. They gave me their maps. It’s all marked.” He bent over and followed the red line with his finger, then smiled encouragingly. Tilly, despite her misgivings, agreed to do it.

]]> In the middle of the night, while everyone was asleep, lying quiet and still and silent; when all the country lay sleeping in their beds,
quietly and undisturbed, there was a thumping, a low steady thumping,
deep, deep down in the deep, deep earth.
No one heard, and no one stirred, and no one knew anything at all until the morning……

There was nothing wrong with their clocks, their radios.
Their neighbours all said the same thing – it was morning.

Everything was as it should be, except
there was no sun
The sun had gone,
vanished! 

So…..What if you woke up and there was no sun. It was still there, but somehow, it was pitch dark. On top of that, there was a weird noise everywhere. Who you gonna call? Probably not a very young boy who knew exactly what the problem was. But did they believe him?

]]> They reckoned that Mica Beha’s family numbered around 200 in all. That was, if you could count them. Mice are hardly ever still, so the count would always have to be a good guess.

Things had become difficult for him, though. It was the human problem again…… 

….when the big beasts saw Mica or his brothers, they never put a foot out of place. The humans! Huh.! They would try to stamp on them on purpose. Yes indeed, it looked as though it was actually on purpose!

This was why Mica was worried. The whole place was filling up more and more each day. ….. 

“Mica!”

He turned. Lumi was tugging at his tail, shouting in a hoarse whispering squeak.

“What?” he whispered back.

“I think I’m going to start soon.”

Mica looked at her round, fat tummy. So beautiful, so pregnant. His sweet Lumi!

“O.K.,” he said. “There’s something happening though. I don’t like the look of it. Something strange.”

Munro must have travelled at least three feet before collapsing in a tired heap. The kiosk was ten yards away, but it was impossible for him to move any further.

“So you still think you can do it on your own?” Safi said.

“I can, I can,” croaked Munro. “and I don’t need any of your interspatial hyperlinks. I’m human – I can do it!”

It was just as well that no one else but Munro could see Safi. He towered above his human friend like a giant umbrella, his orange hat extending well past Munro’s body. But it still didn’t seem to shelter him from the scorching sun.

Safi handed him a crumpled bus ticket.

“I can’t bear to watch any more. Take this, you idiot. There’s no need to win this bet. You don’t really think I would have paid you, anyway?”

Munro sat up and glared at Safi.
